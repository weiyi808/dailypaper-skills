# Secondary Hypertension Subtypes — Clinical Knowledge Base

This file provides the clinical foundation for `/secondary-hypertension-literature-skill`. Load relevant sections when drafting the Background, Methods (population definition), or Discussion.

---

## 1. Primary Aldosteronism (PA)

**Definition:** Autonomous aldosterone excess independent of renin-angiotensin system.

**Prevalence:** 5–12% of hypertensive patients; up to 20% in resistant hypertension.

**Diagnostic pathway:**
1. Screening: Aldosterone-to-Renin Ratio (ARR); threshold varies by assay (commonly ARR > 30 ng/dL per ng/mL/hr)
2. Confirmatory testing: Salt loading (IV or oral), fludrocortisone suppression, captopril challenge, or saline infusion
3. Subtype differentiation: Adrenal CT (first step); Adrenal Vein Sampling (AVS) is gold standard for lateralization before adrenalectomy

**Clinical features:** Hypokalemia (not universal), resistant hypertension, elevated cardiovascular event risk beyond BP level.

**Key outcomes studied in literature:** CV event rate vs. essential hypertension, cure/partial cure rate post-adrenalectomy, biochemical cure rate, fludrocortisone response.

**Commonly used diagnostic criteria in trials:** Endocrine Society 2016 guidelines; ESC 2023 guidelines.

## 2. Renovascular Hypertension (RVH)

**Definition:** Hypertension caused by renal artery stenosis (RAS) impairing renal perfusion and activating RAAS.

**Causes:**
- Atherosclerotic RAS (ARAS): elderly, high CV risk; bilateral in ~30%
- Fibromuscular dysplasia (FMD): young women, typically unilateral

**Prevalence:** 1–5% of hypertensive patients; higher in resistant/accelerating hypertension.

**Diagnostic imaging:**
- First-line: Renal Doppler Ultrasound (sensitivity ~85%, specificity ~92% in experienced centers)
- Confirmatory: CT angiography (CTA) or MR angiography (MRA)
- Functional: Captopril renal scintigraphy (less used now)
- Reference: Catheter angiography (invasive, gold standard)

**Treatment controversy:** CORAL trial (2014) showed no benefit of renal artery stenting over medical therapy in ARAS — this is the dominant evidence anchor; must be cited and contextualized in any RVH literature review.

**Key outcomes:** BP control, renal function preservation, need for renal replacement therapy, cardiovascular events.

## 3. Renal Parenchymal Hypertension

**Definition:** Hypertension arising from chronic kidney disease (CKD) mechanisms: sodium retention, RAAS activation, sympathetic overactivation, reduced vasodilator production.

**Prevalence:** Most common identifiable cause of secondary hypertension; present in >85% of CKD patients.

**Key diagnostic markers:** eGFR (CKD-EPI formula preferred), ACR (albumin-creatinine ratio), renal ultrasound (size, echogenicity).

**Literature focus areas:** BP targets in CKD (SPRINT sub-analysis, ACCORD, AASK), RAAS blockade in CKD, sodium restriction impact, dialysis-related hypertension.

## 4. Pheochromocytoma and Paraganglioma (PPGL)

**Definition:** Catecholamine-secreting tumors of chromaffin cells; pheochromocytomas arise from adrenal medulla, paragangliomas from extra-adrenal ganglia.

**Prevalence:** 0.1–0.6% of hypertensives; ~4% of adrenal incidentalomas.

**Clinical triad:** Episodic headache, palpitations, diaphoresis — present in only ~50%.

**Biochemical diagnosis:**
- Preferred: Plasma free metanephrines (sensitivity >97%) or 24h urine fractionated metanephrines
- Not recommended as first-line: plasma catecholamines, urine VMA

**Imaging:**
- CT abdomen/pelvis (first-line for localization)
- MRI (preferred in metastatic disease, paraganglioma, or pediatric)
- Functional: ¹²³I-MIBG scintigraphy, ⁶⁸Ga-DOTATATE PET (superior sensitivity)

**Genetic testing:** Mandatory; ~40% have germline mutations (SDHB, SDHD, VHL, RET, NF1, etc.)

**Perioperative management:** Alpha-blockade (phenoxybenzamine or selective alpha-1 blockers) ≥ 10–14 days before surgery — critical safety point.

## 5. Cushing Syndrome

**Definition:** Chronic glucocorticoid excess; endogenous (ACTH-dependent 80%, ACTH-independent 20%) or exogenous.

**Prevalence:** 0.1–0.5% of hypertensives; hypertension present in ~80% of Cushing syndrome.

**Biochemical screening (pick ≥ 1):**
- Late-night salivary cortisol (LNSC) × 2
- 24-hour urinary free cortisol (UFC) × 2
- 1mg overnight dexamethasone suppression test (LDDST)

**Confirmatory and localization:**
- 8mg DST, CRH stimulation test
- Pituitary MRI (for Cushing disease)
- Chest/abdomen CT (for ectopic ACTH, adrenal adenoma)
- Inferior petrosal sinus sampling (IPSS) — gold standard for Cushing disease vs. ectopic

**Mechanism of hypertension:** Mineralocorticoid receptor activation, increased vascular reactivity, RAAS stimulation.

## 6. Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)

**Definition:** Repetitive upper airway collapse during sleep causing intermittent hypoxia, sympathetic surges, and sustained hypertension.

**Prevalence:** Present in 30–50% of resistant hypertension; strongest epidemiologic link.

**Diagnosis:**
- Polysomnography (PSG): gold standard; AHI ≥ 15 events/hr = moderate-severe OSA
- Home sleep apnea testing (HSAT): acceptable in uncomplicated moderate-high pretest probability

**Evidence on CPAP and BP:** Meta-analyses show CPAP reduces BP by ~2–3 mmHg (systolic) on average; larger effect in resistant hypertension, non-dippers, and adherent users (>4h/night).

**Key trials to cite:** HIPARCO RCT (resistant HTN + OSA), meta-analyses by Montesi et al., Bratton et al.

## 7. Drug-Induced Hypertension

**Common drug culprits (must specify in PICO):**

| Drug Class | Mechanism |
|-----------|-----------|
| NSAIDs / COX-2 inhibitors | Sodium retention, blunted prostaglandin vasodilation |
| Oral contraceptives (OCP) | Aldosterone-mediated sodium retention, RAAS activation |
| Sympathomimetics (decongestants, ADHD stimulants) | Direct vasoconstriction |
| Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine) | Renal vasoconstriction, sodium retention |
| VEGF inhibitors (bevacizumab, sunitinib) | Reduced NO production |
| Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents | Increased blood viscosity, vasoconstriction |
| Glucocorticoids | Mineralocorticoid effect, weight gain |
| Cocaine / amphetamines | Sympathomimetic surge |

**Literature review approach:** Typically observational (cohort, case-control, pharmacovigilance); use NOS for RoB; PECO framework.

## 8. Thyroid Disorders

**Hyperthyroidism:** Increased CO, heart rate, systolic BP; diastolic BP often reduced.

**Hypothyroidism:** Diastolic hypertension, increased peripheral vascular resistance, dyslipidemia.

**Diagnosis:** TSH (primary screen), fT4, fT3 if indicated.

**Reversibility:** BP often normalizes with euthyroid state — key outcome variable in literature.

---

## 9. Coarctation of Aorta (CoA)

**Definition:** Congenital narrowing of the aortic arch, typically juxtaductal.

**Adults:** Often diagnosed late; upper limb hypertension + lower limb hypotension (arm-leg gradient >20 mmHg).

**Diagnosis:** Echo (initial), CTA or MRI (definitive).

**Post-repair hypertension:** Persistent in 30–50% even after successful repair — major literature topic.

---

## 10. Monogenic Hypertension Syndromes

**Examples:**
- Liddle syndrome (ENaC gain-of-function): low renin, low aldosterone, hypokalemia, amiloride-sensitive
- Gordon syndrome (PHA2): hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis, low renin
- Apparent mineralocorticoid excess (AME): 11β-HSD2 deficiency
- Glucocorticoid-remediable aldosteronism (GRA/FH-I)
- Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH): 11β-hydroxylase or 17α-hydroxylase deficiency

**Literature note:** Evidence base is primarily case series and small cohorts; GRADE will typically be Very Low for interventional questions.
